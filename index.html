Rain

Rain is probably the most common weather pattern on this list. Rain occurs when there is too much moisture in clouds for them to hold on to, the ground and cloud temperatures are above freezing, so rain is in a liquid state. There are three different types of rain, there is classic rain which is typically 0.02 inches or greater in diameter. There is drizzle which is any rain less than 0.02 inches in diameter. Lastly there is Virga, virga is when the rain is so small that it does not even reach the ground before evaporating.

Snow

Snow is basically the opposite of rain. Snow occurs when the ground and cloud temperatures are under the freezing point of water. Snow might look like a fluffy, fun to play in type of weather but if you’re a driver this type of weather could be very dangerous. In the event of snow thawing and a flash freeze happens overnight ice could make the roads very slick. There is also the possibility of a blizzard. These massive snow storms could result in you being snowed in to your house.

Hail

Sometimes when rain is on it’s way down to the ground it comes across a layer of freezing air which can freeze the raindrops and form varying sizes of hailstones. These hailstones will then fall to the ground in place of the raindrops even if the ground temperature is above freezing. Hail is a common part of severe summer thunderstorms. Hail causes roughly one billion dollars in damages a year across the United States, and falls at speeds of up to 120

Tornados

The root cause of most tornadoes is rapidly rotating thunderstorms known as supercells. Visible columns of air extended from the both of the storm is called a funnel cloud. Funnel clouds only become tornadoes when the funnel cloud makes contact with the ground. Tornadoes are considered to be pretty dangerous because they have the power to tear down buildings and rip trees and the roots straight out of the ground. Tornadoes are different categories based on their wind speeds. An EF-0 is a tornado that has wind speeds of 65 to 85. An EF-1 has wind speeds of 86 to 110. The EF-2 tornado has wind speeds of 111 to 135. An EF-3 tornado has wind gusts of 136 to 165. The EF-4 tornado has wind gusts of 166 to 200. Lastly the rarest tornado the EF-5 has the strongest wind gusts of over 200. If you ever encounter a tornado get to a place that can’t be moved, this could be a storm cellar or just a room in your basement with cement walls such as a furnace room.

Hurricanes

The term hurricane is only used for tropical storms that form over the Atlantic Ocean or Eastern Pacific Ocean. The generic, scientific term for these types of storms is actually tropical cyclone. For a tropical cyclone to form it requires warm ocean water. This means that tropical cyclones can only form above tropical regions where the temperature of the water is at least 80 degrees fahrenheit. The second ingredient for a tropical cyclone is wind. The storm is officially a hurricane when wind speeds reach 74. Like tornadoes there are different categories of hurricanes. There is a Category 1 where wind speeds are between 74 and 95 and damage is usually minimal. Then there is a Category 2 where the wind speeds are normally between 96 and 110 and damage is moderate. A Category 3 has wind speeds of 111 to 129 damages are expected to be extensive. Category 4 hurricanes typically have wind speeds of 130 to 156 mph damages in a Cat 4 are usually extreme. Lastly is the Category 5 hurricane wind speeds are 157 mph or higher damages in a Cat 5 are expected to be catastrophic. If you encounter a hurricane stay indoors because the high wind speeds can blow objects around causing hazard to you. If ever told to evacuate do so immediately.
